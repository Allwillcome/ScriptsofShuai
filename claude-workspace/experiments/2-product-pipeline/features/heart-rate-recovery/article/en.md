# About Heart Rate Recovery

## What Is Heart Rate Recovery?

Heart Rate Recovery (HRR) refers to the rate at which the heart rate decreases from its peak during intense exercise toward the resting heart rate. In exercise science, HRR is typically evaluated by measuring the drop in heart rate within 2 minutes after exercise stops. It is an important indicator of cardiovascular health and autonomic nervous system function.

## Why Heart Rate Recovery Matters

HRR reflects not only how well an individual adapts to exercise load, but also serves as an effective tool for assessing overall health and predicting mortality risk. HRR has a strong inverse relationship with fitness level — the higher the aerobic capacity, the faster the heart rate tends to recover. A delayed or reduced HRR may indicate decreased vagal activity, which is a signal of increased cardiovascular disease risk. Specifically, a recovery of fewer than 12 beats per minute within 2 minutes after intense exercise is generally considered abnormal, and may be associated with lower fitness and poorer cardiovascular health.

In addition, monitoring HRR can help physicians and coaches assess an individual's adaptation to training loads and physical condition. Athletes with faster post-exercise heart rate recovery are generally considered to have better aerobic capacity and cardiovascular health.

By monitoring HRR, you can better design training plans, optimize exercise intensity, and take appropriate preventive measures to reduce cardiovascular disease risk.

## Why Isn't Heart Rate Recovery Shown After Every Workout?

HRR is a metric that measures cardiovascular recovery after high-intensity exercise. PeakWatch only calculates and displays this value when your heart rate reaches Z4 (high-intensity aerobic) or above during a workout — only then does the post-exercise heart rate decline carry meaningful physiological significance.

During lower-intensity activities (such as easy walking or stretching), the peak heart rate is not high enough to produce a meaningful recovery curve, and displaying an HRR value in such cases could be misleading.

## Why Does Apple Fitness Show "Post-Workout Heart Rate" but PeakWatch Doesn't?

The "Post-Workout Heart Rate" displayed in the Apple Fitness app is an objective instantaneous heart rate reading at a specific moment after exercise ends — it reflects "what the heart rate is right now."

PeakWatch's Heart Rate Recovery (HRR) measures how much the heart rate drops within 2 minutes after exercise stops — it reflects "how much the heart rate has recovered." The two concepts are different: the former is a snapshot value, while the latter is the scientifically meaningful metric for assessing cardiovascular aerobic recovery capacity.

## Why Does Garmin Show Heart Rate Recovery but PeakWatch Sometimes Doesn't?

Garmin does not provide raw Heart Rate Recovery data. When the raw data signal quality is poor, or the workout data does not meet the physiological requirements for HRR calculation, PeakWatch will choose not to display the value rather than present a potentially inaccurate result. This ensures that every data point you see is trustworthy.
